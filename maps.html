Maps

The process of getting to this set of more conventional maps was meant to be a disorienting one. Prisoners at Eastern State were purposefully given very little in the way of what we might call bird’s eye knowledge, since the entire basis of solitary confinement depended on prisoners having little to no contact with other prisoners and the outside world. Prisoners were hooded when first admitted and led to the their cell so they could not get sense of the prison’s layout, and each cell was soundproof and completely out of sight from other cells. All of the original cells included a small outdoors exercise yard sealed off from other cells and yards so that prisoners could spend time outside without being able to conspire with other prisoners, and hoods and blindfolds continued to be used to transport prisoners within the building.

Eastern State Penitentiary is often noted not only for its innovations in prison management, but also for its contribution to the history of prison architecture. John Haviland, the architect behind the building, was chosen after a nationwide search, and his “wagon wheel” shaped design was notably not fully panoptic, but instead relied on the utter isolation of each prisoner for its effectiveness at keeping them in line (Johnston 31). Not only did the building enforce this isolation spatially, it also did so sonically. One prison administrator described the prison as “a monastery inhabited by men who do not choose to be monks” (qtd. in Johnston 47). Guards wore thick wool socks over their shoes to prevent the sound of their walking from traveling, leather was used on the wheels of carts, and silence from prisoners was enforced at all times (Eastern State Penitentiary Historic Structures Report Volume II 389).

Prisoners would have had even less of a sense of the personal histories of their fellow prisoners, since even those who were arrested together or who knew each other outside of the prison would have no contact with each other whatsoever. Note: The phrase “sweet cell of virtue” comes from Act 1 of Titus Andronicus. In the scene containing the phrase, Titus is lamenting the loss of his sons in battle and refers to a burial plot as that sweet cell. Aside from the obvious invocation of a prison cell, the phrase also feels appropriate to this project in its moralization and romanticization of the cell as a unit.
